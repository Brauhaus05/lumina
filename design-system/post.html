The Brutalist
Resurgence

Concrete is back — and this time, it isn’t apologising. A field report on why a generation of architects, photographers, and quiet city walkers have started paying attention to the unloved bones of the post‑war city.

Fig. 01 · Kessler Foundation, North Facade Photo · B.A.

I.Honesty in Structure

The philosophy of brutalism was never merely about concrete; it was an ethical stance. It demanded that a building reveal its structural truths rather than hide behind decorative veneers. Today’s practitioners are rediscovering this ethos, applying it not just to municipal structures but to high‑end residential and commercial spaces — the places we live, the places we work, the small civic rooms in between.

To design without ornament is to strip away the falsehoods. What remains is the pure dialogue between space, light, and gravity. — Field notebook, March 2024

Consider the recent unveiling of the Kessler Foundation headquarters. The architects eschewed traditional cladding entirely, opting instead for structural elements that perform dual roles as both support and aesthetic focus. The resulting grid‑like facade reads like a blueprint brought into three physical dimensions — a building that has not bothered to dress up for the meeting.

II.The Grid as Canvas

Within this paradigm, the grid becomes the supreme organising principle. It is not something to be concealed, but celebrated. By exposing the structural framework, designers create a visual rhythm that grounds the occupant. This approach requires meticulous planning, as every junction, every poured seam, becomes a visible design choice — and a permanent one.

III.Field Notes & Details

What is striking, walking these buildings, is how loud silence can be. Concrete absorbs and returns sound differently than glass. A footstep in a Le Corbusier stairwell does not echo so much as it confesses. The body adjusts: shoulders lower, conversation hushes, the eye starts to read the wall like text.

The colour, too, surprises. We learn to call concrete grey because photography flattens it, but in person it is closer to wet stone, or unbleached linen, or the particular off‑white of an envelope left on a sunny windowsill for a week. It changes by the hour.

IV.What Comes Next

As we move forward, this brutalist resurgence challenges us to reconsider what we define as ‘premium.’ It suggests that true luxury lies not in applied decoration, but in the masterful execution of essential forms — in the enduring presence of architectural mass, and the quiet confidence of a building that does not need to be liked.
